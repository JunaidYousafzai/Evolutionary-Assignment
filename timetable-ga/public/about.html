The Science

Swarm Intelligence Architecture

Discover how we use the collective behavior of particle swarms to solve complex mathematical constraint satisfaction problems.

1. Swarm Initialization

The algorithm creates a swarm of particles, each representing a completely random timetable. Each particle has a position (the timetable solution) and a velocity (the tendency to change). Most initial solutions are terrible and filled with clashes, but they form the starting exploration space for the swarm.

2. Fitness Evaluation

Every particle's position is graded using a rigorous fitness function. It loses points for teacher double-bookings, room overlaps, and capacity violations. The closer the score is to 1.0, the better. Each particle remembers the best position it has ever found (personal best, or pBest).

3. Velocity Update

Each particle adjusts its velocity based on three forces: Inertia (tendency to keep moving in its current direction), Cognitive Pull (attraction toward its own personal best solution), and Social Pull (attraction toward the global best solution found by any particle in the swarm). These forces combine to guide the search.

4. Position Update

Based on the updated velocity, each particle moves to a new position in the solution space — effectively creating a new timetable. A small random exploration factor prevents the swarm from getting stuck in a local optimum, ensuring global coverage of the solution landscape.

5. Convergence

This process repeats hundreds of times per second. As particles share information about the best solutions they've found, the entire swarm converges on a globally optimal solution: a schedule with a perfect fitness score of 1.0 and zero clashes.
